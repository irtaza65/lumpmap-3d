We often call any unexplained bump a cyst. But cyst, daana, phinsi, boil, and gilti can describe very different structures—and different levels of urgency.

Cyst-related concerns affected me personally. I built the tool I wish I had then: visual, private, and honest about what it can and cannot know.

LumpMap 3D is not a diagnostic tool. It is an anatomy-first educational navigator. The journey begins with one useful clue: location.

Choose from seventeen body regions through a rotatable 3D body or searchable accessible list. Selecting the top of the buttock cleft does not return a diagnosis; it opens patterns worth learning about.

Across fourteen curated lessons, the atlas changes from body scale to beneath-the-skin scale. For pilonidal disease, three stages show a normal cleft, a pit and sinus tract, and inflamed disease—labelled as possible progression, not a prediction.

Comparison mode holds the tissue scale constant, so a pilonidal sinus, a boil, and an epidermoid cyst can be understood side by side.

If a user is not sure where to begin, they can describe what they noticed in English, Urdu, Roman Urdu, or use guided questions.

With a server key, GPT-5.6 normalizes everyday wording into a strict structured symptom record. It does not diagnose, generate medical content, select matches, or decide urgency. The no-key demo and guided flow keep the safety experience testable.

For this Roman Urdu example, local matching recognizes a recurring painful armpit pattern and places prompt-appointment guidance before curated comparisons, including HS. The Visit Note contains only user-supplied facts.

Now compare painful perianal swelling with fever. Deterministic local rules raise same-day urgent guidance and show the exact trigger facts. GPT-5.6 cannot lower that result.

I built LumpMap with Codex and GPT-5.6: the interface, procedural Three.js anatomy, typed safety rules, accessibility, privacy protections, and fifty-six focused tests. Every lesson shows sources and review dates, with no accounts, trackers, photo uploads, or saved health history. Learn the pattern, not a label.
